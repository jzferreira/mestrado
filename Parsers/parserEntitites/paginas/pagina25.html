Nokia Lumia 800 lights Windows way

November 22, 2011 11:13 pm by FT
 

Nokia has much resting on the launch of its first Windows smartphone, the Lumia 800, the first off the production line since its exclusive partnership was agreed with Microsoft in February. The phone is key for Nokia to claw back a position in high-end phones having been marginalised by Apple and Google’s Android in recent years, writes Daniel Thomas,  FT telecoms correspondent.

Nokia makes big play about simply being able to produce a device in the nine months since striking the deal, and perhaps with good reason, given its product cycles had tended to be over-extended by infinite Scandinavian design debate.

Indeed, even the concept of a single flagship product to lead the line is against the Nokian tradition of throwing out entire ranges of devices at a myriad of price points in the hope that a few would stick. It was no great surprise when, at the Nokia World launch in October, the company could not resist a sneaky second launch of a cut price smart phone for emerging markets.

With all this in mind, there are reasons to admire the 800, which is a good looking phone that should give the firm a seat back at the table in the European smartphone market, but in truth there are no great surprises in Nokia’s opening gambit.

Nokia has in effect stapled the existing Windows Mango 7.5 operating system to the hardware already developed for the previous generation smartphone, the N9, which had perhaps had the shortest shelf life of any major phone launch in being superseded in just a few weeks by the 800.

The good news is that the handset itself remains as impressive as it was for the N9. It is a solid and nicely weighted piece of polycarbon with an excellent camera and a curved screen that highlights the black dropped tiled Windows system and accentuates a vivid photo and video quality.

The phone looks and feels good, but whether you actually like using it will depend on your view of the Windows operating system, which for me still lacks the high-end applications of Apple and Android. Microsoft appears to be getting more out of this relationship than Nokia so far judging by the lack of innovation in the Windows system, although Nokians are quick to point out that this is early in their relationship and bespoke services can be expected in future generations of their phones.

This will be crucial to give it an edge over the other Windows phones, regardless of the other systems, although there are already minor annoyances in having to tie on some of the obligatory Windows functions. The hardwired Bing search function, for example, which is unlikely the first choice for most users.

The music and movie functions are slightly clumsy, without the intuitive one-click iPod functions of the Apple devices, although social media is well integrated, and again in particular for Microsoft’s Windows Live or Hotmail users.

Similarly, I would imagine that it would be helpful to have an Xbox Live account, although the suite of Microsoft Office apps stand out as particularly useful even if tablet owners will have few reasons to do much work on the mobile.

The Drive function works well using Nokia’s unique mapping technology, but this can be more or less replicated by anyone with a phone and Google Maps, and indeed Nokia has agreed to share it with other makers of Windows phones.

I tested the phone prior to launch and it would also appear to have a few bugs – for example, the marketplace for apps and content, which was otherwise effective, refused to accept that I had no updates to download.

The 800 is a decent first step back into the smartphone market, but it is to be hoped that Nokia is as hard at work, as the rumours would suggest,  on new products that offer a more unique experience. There is only a marginal pricing difference from the high-end Apple and Android phones already on the market. That alone will not drive the volume necessary to create a true competitor.

There is talk of a larger screen 900 model and a tablet using the Windows 8 operating system in the New Year. Both would begin to create a momentum behind the brand that the 800 on its own would struggle to match.  NFC (near field communication), which was part of the N9, is also strangely lacking, but future models are expected to feature the mobile payment technology.

Stephen Elop, chief executive of Nokia, last week looked slightly disgruntled when I said that the phone currently rated a 6.5 out of ten, but there would not have to be much done to the Lumia to raise that score much higher.

It feels to me Mr Elop was being slightly disingenuous when he described the Lumia as “the first real Windows Phone” – perhaps against its existing competition, but a real phone marrying the best of both companies is still to come. The hard work on the form has been done, but there needs to be a better grasp of the basic integration with function for the partnership between Nokia and Microsoft to work.
